Everyone in education is talking about AI right now. Most of it is noise. Here's how I actually think about it after using these tools every day to run a tuition business.

## The hype

The pitch is seductive: an AI tutor for every student, available 24/7, infinitely patient, for almost nothing. If you only read the headlines, you'd think human teachers are about to be replaced. I don't believe that — and I run a business that would benefit enormously if it were true.

What I've learned is that AI changes the _shape_ of teaching far more than it changes who does it.

## What actually works

The wins are real and they're unglamorous. AI is excellent at the work around teaching: generating practice questions, marking drills, drafting feedback, spotting which student is quietly falling behind. That's the busywork that used to eat a tutor's evenings.

> When you give a teacher back ten hours a week, they spend it on the thing only they can do — actually understanding the student in front of them.

That's the leverage. Not replacing the teacher, but removing everything that isn't teaching.

## What it still can't do

AI doesn't notice when a kid is distracted because something happened at home. It can't read the room, build trust over months, or know when to push and when to back off. Motivation — the hardest and most important part of learning — is still deeply human.

It also confidently gets things wrong, which is dangerous in a setting where a student doesn't yet know enough to catch the mistake.

## My take

So the bet I'm making isn't "AI instead of teachers." It's "a small team of great teachers, each amplified by AI, beating a large team without it." That's the business I'm building — and so far, the results back it up.

If you're working on something similar, I'd love to compare notes.
