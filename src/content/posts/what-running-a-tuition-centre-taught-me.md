When I started, I thought the centre would live or die on the quality of the teaching. It turns out teaching is the part I already knew how to do. Everything around it was the real test.

## The easy part

Standing in front of a class and explaining a hard idea well — that's a craft, and it's learnable. Most tutors who care get good at it. It's necessary, but it was never the bottleneck.

## The hidden work

Scheduling. Chasing feedback. Knowing which student hasn't shown up in two weeks. Onboarding a new tutor without losing the standard. None of it is glamorous, and all of it quietly decides whether the business grows or grinds.

> A centre doesn't fail because the teaching is bad. It fails because nobody noticed the small things slipping until they'd become big things.

## Systems win

The fix was boring and it worked: write the process down, then automate the parts a person shouldn't have to remember. Once the operations ran themselves, the teaching had room to be great again.

That's the lesson I keep relearning — build the system, and the teaching takes care of itself.
